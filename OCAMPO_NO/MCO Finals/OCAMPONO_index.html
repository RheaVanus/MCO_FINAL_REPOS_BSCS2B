The history of Folk
Dance in the Philippines

Pre-colonial

Pre-colonial dances are distinctly meant to appease the Gods and to ask favors from spirits, as a means to celebrate their harvest or hunt. Their dance mimicked life forms and the stories of their community. Prior to the arrival of foreign influences, the nation had a well-developed heritage of cultural dances drawing its roots from animistic rituals, communal celebrations, and preparation for war. Pre-colonial dances played crucial roles in ceremonies, storytelling, and social bonding as artistic expressions.

Spanish Colonial Era

The arrival of Spanish colonizers in the 16th century changed the course of Philippine folk dance considerably. Fusions of European and indigenous forms of dance created a new category of dances. Christianized Filipinos, now under colonial powers, used to perform dances revolving around courtship and festival dances. Despite the pre-colonial people having them type of government, writing, myths, and traditions, several features of Hispanic culture have influenced different aspects of today's Filipino culture, from clothing, such as the barong Tagalog and the terno, to their religion even up to the dances and music.

American Occupation Era

Similarly, as in Spanish colonization, in 1898 the Americans brought their own commercial and worldwide culture which had impacted the Filipinos as well. The American colonial period in the early 20th century introduced aspects of modernization that had an impact on folk dances. After World War II, folk dances became instruments of cultural preservation and nationalistic sentiment.

Some of the popular
Folk Dances in the Philippines

Alitaptap

Filipino folk dance alitaptap or firefly dance is derived from Batangas and its native Tagalogs. The dance imitates the flashing motions of fireflies in the form of complex foot movements and hand signals—twinkling on and off like its namesake creature.

Arigay

The arigay folk dance is a spirited courtship ritual that mimics movements of roosters and hens. The many kicks and waving of arms in the dance need evasive poise and coordination to make the movements look uncontrolled.

Binasuan

Binasuan literally means 'use/have drinking glasses' in Pangasinan province dialect. It is a balance test for its dancers since the prop glasses they carry are at least half full of a drink. These glasses are put skillfully in the dancer's head and hands, so they have to keep balance very well while swinging their arms wide circles.

Carinosa

Cariñosa is a Filipino dance which falls under the Maria Clara collection of Philippine folk dances. The dance has beautiful choreography and makes use of Spanish colonial costumes and props like handkerchiefs or hand fans.

Itik-itik

Itik-itik is a playful paired Filipino folk dance which imitates the gait of a duck. Dancers portray a teasing and courtship narrative in gracious and capricious steps.

La Jota Moncadena

The Filipino people's dance Lla Jota Moncadena beautifully blends Filipino flair with Spanish grace. Dancers, typically male-female couples, execute elegant ballroom-style footwork. It's one of the only castanet-percussing Filipino folk dances—a Spanish percussion tool.

Maglalatik

Filipino war dance maglalatik involves coconut shells and symbolizes the war between colonizers and Moros for latik. In this folk dance, the sound of colliding coconuts imitates war, and dancers skillfully execute body movement like instruments.

Pandanggo sa Ilaw

Pandanggo sa ilaw is a visually stunning dance that involves balancing light lamps or candles on the head and hands of the dancers. The Filipino folk dance with candles showcases the dancers' agility and elegance in an excellent display of lights.

Salakot

Coming from Mindanao, the salakot Philippine folk dance employs salakot hats, usually with colorful motifs. The dancers usually incorporate movements to attract attention to the salakot hats that they are bearing.

Some Photos of Filipino Folk Dances

Filipino folk dances are more than just movements, they are stories of our ancestors, expressions of our values, and living pieces of our heritage. I love how each dance reflects the spirit, resilience, and joy of the Filipino people. Whether it's the grace of Pandanggo sa Ilaw, the strength of Tinikhado, or the celebration of community in Tinikling, these dances remind me of the beauty and richness of our culture. I care deeply about preserving and honoring these traditions so that future generations can continue to feel the same pride, connection, and love for our roots.
